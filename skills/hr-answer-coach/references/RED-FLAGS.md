# Red-Flag Pattern Catalog

Patterns an HR person actually reacts to, with the inference each one
triggers. Use this as a checklist when scanning a draft answer — not every
pattern applies to every question, and a clean draft should not be forced
to match one.

## 1. Victim framing

**Phrases:** «меня сократили», «меня уволили», «попал под сокращение»,
«компания закрыла направление, и я остался без работы»

**HR inference:** Candidate is positioning themselves as something that
happened *to* them rather than someone in control of their career. Reads
as "признали лишним" even when the layoff was purely economic and not
performance-related. Shifts the candidate into a begging/asking posture
instead of an equal-value posture.

**Fix direction:** State the fact neutrally and move past it fast — "моё
сотрудничество с [компанией] завершилось" / "проект, в котором я работал,
закрылся" — then pivot immediately to what comes next. Don't dwell.

## 2. Disloyalty / complaint signals

**Phrases:** «были ограничения на работу из РФ», «не давали расти»,
«не устраивала политика компании», «постоянно сидели сверхурочно»

**HR inference:** "Этот человек будет жаловаться, если мы не дадим ему то,
что он хочет." Complaining about a previous employer — even when factually
accurate — reads as a preview of how the candidate will talk about *this*
company in six months. RU-market specific: hinting at wanting to escape
"ограничения" tied to RF status can read as politically loaded and, for a
Russian-only employer, as a loyalty question mark.

**Fix direction:** Drop the complaint entirely unless directly asked.
Reframe around what you're moving *toward*, not what you're escaping.

## 3. Manifesto / arrogance

**Phrases:** «планирую развивать инженерную культуру у вас», «хочу принести
вам процессы, которых тут не хватает», «приду и научу команду»

**HR inference:** Candidate is positioning themselves above the company —
missionary, not hire. HR is looking for someone who solves *their*
problems, not someone who arrives with a mandate to fix the company's
culture. Reads as impractical and presumptuous, especially from someone who
hasn't worked there yet.

**Fix direction:** Reframe as an offer, not a mission — "у меня есть опыт
с X, и если для вас это релевантно, готов делиться" — contribution
conditional on their need, not a unilateral plan.

## 4. Cliché / passive motivation

**Phrases:** «ищу стабильности», «хочу интересные задачи», «нужен дружный
коллектив», «хочу развития»

**HR inference:** Everyone wants this — it's not a differentiator and
signals the candidate hasn't actually thought about why *this* role/company
specifically. Also reads as passive: "дайте мне стабильность" puts the
company in the position of providing, the candidate in the position of
receiving, rather than the reverse.

**Fix direction:** Replace with something specific to this role/company
that took thought to identify — a problem they're solving that matches the
candidate's actual track record.

## 5. Mercenary / salary-only motivation

**Phrases:** «у вас выше зарплата», «предложили лучше условия» (as the
*entire* answer, with nothing else)

**HR inference:** Not wrong to care about compensation, but if it's the
*only* stated reason, HR reads the candidate as a flight risk who will
leave the moment a better offer appears.

**Fix direction:** Compensation can be one factor among several — pair it
with a substantive reason (scope, problem, growth) so it doesn't stand
alone.

## 6. Conflict/negativity about people

**Phrases:** «не сошлись характерами с руководителем», «команда была
токсичной», «меня не ценили»

**HR inference:** Even if true, this is read as a preview of how the
candidate will describe *this* team later, and raises the question of
whether the candidate contributed to the conflict. HR cannot verify the
other side of the story, so they discount in the candidate's favor by
default — meaning the story makes the candidate look worse, not the former
employer.

**Fix direction:** For behavioral/conflict questions specifically (where
this is *the* topic asked about), use the STAR framework and keep language
neutral and action-focused — describe what *you* did to address it, not
what was wrong with the other person.

## 7. Oversharing / unscoped detail

**Phrases:** long answers that wander into unrelated personal,
financial, or political detail not asked for.

**HR inference:** Signals poor self-editing and weak read of what's
relevant in a professional context — a proxy for how the candidate will
communicate at work (status updates, incident reports, etc.).

**Fix direction:** Cut to the structure relevant to the question being
asked (see ANSWER-STRUCTURE.md). If in doubt, shorter.

## 8. Underselling / over-apologizing

**Phrases:** «у меня не так много опыта, но...», «я не уверен, что
подхожу, но решил попробовать»

**HR inference:** Candidate is pre-emptively lowering the bar for
themselves — reads as low confidence, and primes the interviewer to look
for reasons to reject rather than reasons to hire.

**Fix direction:** State qualifications factually without the hedge. Let
the interviewer ask follow-up questions about gaps rather than volunteering
doubt.

## RU-market specific notes

- Mentioning sanctions, emigration, or "работа из РФ" restrictions reads
  differently depending on the target company (see SKILL.md Step 2): a
  Russian-only employer may read it as a loyalty signal risk; an
  international or remote-friendly company may read it neutrally as a
  practical fact. Calibrate severity to the answer from Step 2, don't
  apply a blanket rule.
- Avoid any phrasing that implies political commentary, even indirectly —
  HR in this market reliably treats it as a risk regardless of the
  candidate's actual intent.
