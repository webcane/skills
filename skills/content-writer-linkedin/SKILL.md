# LinkedIn Content Writer Skill

A structured approach to creating authentic LinkedIn articles that resonate with professional audiences by sharing genuine experiences and practical lessons. Combines personal narrative with accessible, engaging writing and optional research verification.

## Three-Mode Operation

This skill operates in three sequential modes. You can skip Research/Verify if your article is purely personal narrative.

### Mode 1: OUTLINE
Create the structure before writing. Answer planning questions, sketch the 6-part narrative, and define word count targets for each section.

**Output:** A markdown outline saved to your workspace showing:
- Title (max 70 characters, sentence case, attention-grabbing)
- 6 sections with descriptions of content
- Word count targets per section
- Key transitions and story beats

### Mode 2: RESEARCH/VERIFY (Optional - Skip by Default)
Only needed if your article includes data, statistics, trends, or claims that need verification.

**When to use:**
- Your article cites specific numbers or percentages
- You're discussing industry trends or events
- You make claims that could be fact-checked
- You want to ensure data accuracy

**When to skip:**
- Pure personal narrative and experience sharing
- Your story doesn't rely on external facts
- You're confident in your knowledge (you lived it or know it well)

**Output:** Verified facts, sources, and integration points where research supports your narrative.

### Mode 3: WRITE
Draft the article section by section, following the outline and style guidelines. After writing, move to the Review loop.

**Output:** Complete drafted article ready for review and revision.

## The LinkedIn Article Formula

### Core Principles

**Minto Pyramid Structure**: Lead with your main insight or conclusion, then build the logical argument that supports it.

**Authenticity First**: Write from genuine personal experience. The best LinkedIn articles share a real challenge and how you actually solved it—not how you think others should solve it.

**Human Narrative**: Your article should read like a thoughtful conversation with a professional peer, not a corporate memo or marketing piece.

**Usefulness**: The goal is to share something readers can learn from or apply to their own work. It's not about promoting yourself, your company, or selling anything.

### The 6-Part Structure

1. **Personal Context or Observation** (Opening)
   - Start with a moment, observation, or situation that sets up the story
   - Make it specific enough that readers recognize themselves in it
   - This is where you establish credibility through relatability, not credentials

2. **The Problem** (Conflict)
   - Clearly name the challenge you faced
   - Explain why it mattered to you
   - Help readers understand why this problem is worth paying attention to

3. **Why the Conventional Approach Didn't Work** (Complication)
   - Explain what you tried first or what others typically do
   - Be honest about why the obvious solution fell short
   - This is where you show critical thinking—you didn't just accept the standard playbook

4. **What Actually Helped** (Resolution)
   - Describe the approach, shift, or realization that worked
   - Walk through your thinking process
   - Include mistakes you made along the way—these make the narrative credible

5. **The Practical Lesson** (Takeaway)
   - Extract the insight readers can apply themselves
   - Connect it back to the broader professional challenge
   - Avoid generic advice; be specific enough to be useful

6. **Invitation for Discussion** (Closing)
   - End with a question or open-ended thought that invites comments
   - This drives engagement and shows you want dialogue, not just an audience

## Style Guidelines

### Readability & Engagement

**Target Reading Level:** Flesch-Kincaid Grade 6-8 (accessible to all LinkedIn users, not just subject-matter experts)

**Sentence Rhythm:** Vary sentence length dramatically for engagement and pacing:
- Mix short sentences (5-10 words) for impact
- Medium sentences (15-20 words) for context
- Longer sentences (25+ words) for complex ideas
- Example pattern: Short. Medium sentence here. Then a longer sentence that provides additional context and nuance while flowing naturally.

**Paragraph Length:** Keep paragraphs short (2-4 sentences max). LinkedIn readers scan—don't make them work for it.

**Words Per Section:** See length recommendations below for targets.

**Rhythm example:**
"My project management approach was failing. So I tried something radically different. What surprised me was how much the team responded to one small change: asking for their input before finalizing timelines. Suddenly, projects moved faster. They weren't resisting the plan anymore—they were executing it."

---

- **Write in first person** ("I discovered," "I learned," "I tried")
- **Use your authentic voice** — write like you speak to colleagues
- **Show your thinking** — explain the reasoning, not just the conclusion
- **Include specific details** — concrete examples beat abstract statements
- **Build natural transitions** — let ideas flow logically from one to the next
- **Be conversational** — use contractions, varied sentence length, human pacing
- **Admit limitations** — sharing what didn't work or what you still don't know builds trust
- **Make minor imperfections** — the occasional typo or missing comma proves this is human-written, not AI-generated

### What to Avoid

**Banned Words & Phrases:**
- Buzzwords: "synergy," "leverage," "disrupt," "game-changer," "paradigm shift," "ecosystem," "move the needle," "low-hanging fruit," "deep dive," "at the end of the day"
- AI-style openers: "In today's fast-paced world," "It's important to understand," "Now more than ever," "Remember that," "Based on the information provided"
- Overused fillers: "key takeaway," "unlocking potential," "best practices," "simply," "actually," "essentially," "really," "very," "quite"
- Corporate jargon: "utilize," "leverage," "optimize," "synergistic," "holistic," "robust," "scalable," "stakeholder engagement," "bandwidth," "circle back"
- Pretentious words: "delve," "tapestry," "vibrant," "landscape," "realm," "embark," "excels," "vital," "comprehensive," "intricate," "pivotal," "notably," "arguably," "crucial," "encompass"
- Vague instructions: "Dive into," "Navigating the," "Delving into," "A testament to," "Understanding," "Establishing," "Effectively," "Significantly," "Accelerate," "Consider"
- Weak endings: "In conclusion," "In summary," "To conclude"

**Formatting to Avoid:**
- Em dashes (—) — use parentheses or shorter sentences instead
- Colons in headings
- Headings that start with numbers
- Exaggerated claims or unverified information
- H3 headings unless absolutely necessary
- Bullet lists unless they genuinely improve clarity (LinkedIn favors prose)

## The Planning Phase

Before you write, work through these questions:

**About Your Topic:**
- What specific experience or insight do you want to share?
- What problem does this insight address?
- Who would benefit most from understanding this?

**About Your Audience:**
- What's their professional context? (industry, role, level of experience)
- What challenges do they face related to your topic?
- What would they find useful or surprising?

**About Your Angle:**
- What's the counterintuitive or underappreciated part of your insight?
- Why does this matter now?
- What do you know that others might not?

**About Your Story:**
- What specifically happened that taught you this lesson?
- What did you try first?
- What surprised you about the process?

## Image Guidance

LinkedIn articles perform better with a visual. Recommend one of these approaches:

- **A professional photo of the author** (wearing authentic workplace attire, not posed)
- **A visual that illustrates the core concept** (e.g., a moment from the story, a relevant scene)
- **A design that frames the key insight** (minimal, clean, readable as a thumbnail)

Avoid stock photos unless they feel genuinely relevant. LinkedIn audiences can spot generic imagery from miles away.

## Title Guidance

Your title should:
- Be specific about what readers will learn
- Avoid clickbait or sensationalism
- Use active language when possible
- Be between 7-12 words ideally

**Strong titles:**
- "Why Your Roadmap Fails (And What I Do Instead)"
- "The Meeting That Changed How I Listen"
- "What Happens When You Stop Following the Plan"

**Weak titles:**
- "An Interesting Discovery"
- "Lessons in Leadership"
- "My Journey"

## Length Recommendations

### Overall Article Lengths

- **Short form** (250-400 words): A sharp story with a clear lesson. Good for busy professionals.
- **Medium form** (400-700 words): Room for more nuance, detail, and reflection. Most engaging.
- **Long form** (700-1000+ words): Deep exploration of a complex idea with multiple examples. Only if the story really warrants it.

LinkedIn's algorithm favors content that gets comments, not length. A tight 400-word article that sparks discussion often outperforms a rambling 1000-word piece.

### Word Count Targets Per Section

Use these as guidelines when outlining your article:

1. **Opening (Personal Context):** 50-75 words
   - Hook the reader immediately with a specific moment
   - No lengthy setup needed

2. **Problem Statement:** 75-100 words
   - Clearly name what went wrong
   - Explain why it mattered

3. **Why Conventional Approach Failed:** 100-150 words
   - Show what you tried first
   - Explain the gap or limitation

4. **What Actually Worked:** 150-200 words
   - Most substantive section
   - Walk through the solution with specifics

5. **Practical Lesson (Takeaway):** 100-125 words
   - Extract the insight readers can apply
   - Connect back to broader challenge

6. **Closing & Discussion Invitation:** 25-50 words
   - Wrap up briefly
   - End with a genuine question

**Total target:** 500-700 words (medium form)

## Review & Revision Loop

After the draft is complete, you enter the **collaborative review phase**. This is where the article gets polished through targeted rephrasing.

### How the Review Loop Works

1. Present the full article with clear section breaks
2. Rephrase only the specific parts the user requests — sentences, paragraphs, sections, or tone adjustments
3. After each revision, ask "Which parts would you like me to improve?"
4. Continue until the user signals completion ("Done", "Looks good", or similar)

### Review Loop Guidelines

- **Be specific** — Instead of "Make it better," try "This paragraph is too long and has too much jargon"
- **Focus on one or two areas at a time** — Don't overwhelm with too many changes at once
- **You're in control** — I won't push for changes you don't want
- **Ask me for suggestions** — I'll offer improvement ideas, but only implement what you request
- **Iterate as many times as needed** — There's no limit to refinement passes

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**The Humble-Brag**: "I'm so grateful for my success, here's why you can't have it"
→ Instead, focus on what didn't work and what you learned from failure.

**The Abstract Lesson**: "You should be authentic and trust yourself"
→ Instead, show through your story what authenticity or trust looked like in action.

**The One-Size-Fits-All Advice**: "Every leader needs to do X"
→ Instead, share what worked for you in your specific context, and let readers adapt it.

**The Invisible Author**: Content so polished it sounds like a company published it, not a person
→ Let your voice and personality come through. Imperfection is proof of authenticity.

## Checklist Before Publishing

- [ ] Does it open with a specific moment or observation?
- [ ] Is the problem clearly named?
- [ ] Have I explained why the conventional approach didn't work?
- [ ] Is my solution described with enough detail to be useful?
- [ ] Does it feel like genuine experience, not theoretical advice?
- [ ] Are there any buzzwords or corporate clichés?
- [ ] Would I say this the same way to a colleague over coffee?
- [ ] Does it end with a real question, not a sales pitch?
- [ ] Is the image relevant and authentic?
- [ ] Have I added a title that promises a specific insight?
- [ ] Does it follow sentence rhythm variation (short, medium, long)?
- [ ] Is it at Flesch-Kincaid grade 6-8 reading level?
